Research

I work at the intersection of History and Science and Technology Studies (STS), examining how scientists, the questions they ask, the technology they use, and the world in which they live in all shape each other. I am interested in scientists’ data practices—how they make their data useable, portable, and intelligible—and what these practices mean about and for the context in which this science happens.

Stated more specifically, and with more STS shibboleths, I use historical and ethnographic methods to examine the infrastructure for circulation of scientific data. I am interested in especially in the implications of open data practices for managing controversies in interdisciplinary spaces.

I wrote my M.A. thesis on some cases where removing detail from genomic data sets made the data more useful and portable (by protecting interests like consumer privacy and intellectual property). I focused on social scientists using genomic data, though many of the practices I examined are broadly relevant in genomics. For my dissertation, I am extending this research, both adding historical context for these practicies and examining the scientific and ethical values embedded in these data practices.

I am also working on a project examining the relationship between open science, reproducibility, and environmental regulation with Nicole Nelson and Kelsey Ichikawa.

On games

For various reasons I’ve ended up making games that live on the browser but encourage interaction with your opponents in other ways, like over email, IRL. (OK, it’s mostly because I’m a noob at web stuff and don’t want to roll my own infrastructure so I’m outsourcing the communication parts.)

Putting the ‘tank’ in ‘think tank’? Emulating a game that physicist George Gamow designed while working at the Army’s Operations Research office around 1950. Joshua from WarGames might call it “a strange game.” You can play Gamow’s tank game here, with some changes for playablity, or read the history here and help me figure out why Gamow designed such an unplayable game in the first place.

Let’s play, grandma! A word-building game I made for my grandma. Much better than the adware smartphone games I used to play with her on. It’s also fun because it's played by passing the game state back-and-forth over email. This constraint makes for an asynchronous, epistilatory play style.

Let’s eat[,] god! Here’s a game I didn’t make, but that I enjoy and you might too. Unlike the others, the computer won’t referee it for you; find some friends and play it in–person!

Occasionally asked questions

Any relation to Apple Inc./Macintosh/Steve Jobs?

Nope! Jobs and Jef Raskin wanted to name the Mac after Raskin’s favorite apple, but misspelled McIntosh to avoid trademark conflicts with McIntosh Laboratory (also no relation). Extraneous A’s have been wandering into my name ever since. I can’t blame them, though: McIntosh apples (still no relation!) are delicious.

What about Jane Austen’s Bennets?

Also nope! There’s no interesting story there, I guess my parents just liked the version with two T’s.

Is your favicon a d20?

It can be if you want! Long ago, when my website was hosted on Squarespace, it was a riff on the cube favicon that platform was using as a default. Several platforms later it’s a dead metaphor, a platonically [heh] empty signifier, begging for any meaning you care to attach to it. But yes, I play enough D&D to think “d20” when I see an icosahedron.
